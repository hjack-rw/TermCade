# Xiaolin Showdown

A Wu is not found. It **reveals itself**, and when it does, everyone who wants it starts running.
When two hands close on a Wu at the same moment, and neither lets go, that is where a Xiaolin Showdown begins.

A Showdown is not a brawl – it is a **rule**, one of Dashi's protections, and the Wu themselves keep it.
Nobody enforces the pact. The pact simply holds.

---

> **Who calls it**

Whoever gets there first.

Some Wu make you faster. Some make you surer on your feet, or harder to slow down, or better at
finding the thing in the first place – and a hunter carrying those arrives ahead of one who isn't.
That head start is what buys you the right to **name the challenge**: which of your strengths the
Showdown will be fought on. Force, or agility, or intellect – or all three at once, if you think you
are able enough.

The one who arrives second gets the rest of the say: **the grounds it is fought on, and the Wu used**.
You set the terms, I set the stakes. Neither of you gets both.

---

> **The Arena**

Where the Wu surfaced does not matter. The moment the challenge is called, the world folds and puts
the two of you **somewhere else entirely** – a place that exists out-of-time only for the fight and for nothing else.
A giant iceberg. A volcano's throat. A field of sunflowers. The deck of a ship.

Nobody built it. Nobody chose it out of a book. It is simply the place the Showdown is going to happen in, 
and it is already extreme when you arrive; the place is not just scenery.

A Wu at home in it moves like it was made for the ground under its feet.
A Wu at odds with it is fighting the ground and its opponent at the same time.

That advantage is not purely elemental – it is everything about being in the right place with the
right thing in your hand. Fire in a volcano is not *stronger*. It is simply where it belongs, and that shows.

When it is over, the world folds back. The lake was never frozen. Like you were never there.

---

> **What it costs**

Each side wagers Wu they already hold, and the loser hands over **every one they staked** – 
that is the whole of the pact, and it is why a wide playfield is a real decision and not a simple one.

> **Gong Yi Tanpai!**

There is no walking away, and no watching what the other one does first. You commit blind, both of
you, in the same instant. A duelist who could wait and answer would never lose, and Dashi was not a fool.

---

> **What it is worth**

Win, and you take what they staked.

Take the **revealed Wu** as well – the one you were both racing for – and you must win *decisively*.
A narrow victory settles who kept their own. Only a real blow takes the prize off the ground.

There are other ways to earn it. A win that is broad rather than deep will do it: strong in two of
the three, or commanding in all of them. And a duelist who fought in tune with the ground has a claim
of their own – the Wu answers to whoever belonged there, even if they won ugly. 

---

> **And the Wu nobody took**

It is not destroyed. It is **lost**.

Two people fought over it and neither closed a hand on it hard enough to keep it, so it went
where Dashi's Wu go when nobody is wielding them – back to sleep, unaccounted for, waiting.

It will surface again – one day, for somebody. Perhaps even for you.
